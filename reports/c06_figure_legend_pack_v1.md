## Figure C06-1

### Full Title

Tea polyphenols may reduce obesity-related phenotypes through microbiota remodeling, SCFA-linked signaling, and barrier protection

### Short Title

Polyphenol-microbiota-barrier-obesity path

### Caption Draft

**Figure C06-1. A graph-derived mechanistic path linking tea polyphenols to obesity-related phenotypes through microbiota remodeling, SCFA-linked signaling, and barrier protection.**  
The C06 subgraph extracted from the refined tea evidence database highlights a multi-hop route connecting tea polyphenols to obesity-related outcomes. In the current evidence layer, tea polyphenols are linked to reported shifts in taxa including Blautia and Faecalibaculum, together with reduced Colidextribacter. These microbiota changes co-occur with SCFA-associated signaling and host processes involving G protein-coupled receptor activation, histone deacetylase inhibition, tight-junction enhancement, and intestinal barrier preservation. Downstream, these mechanism nodes connect to reduced inflammatory burden and obesity-related phenotypes in high-fat-diet mouse models. Provenance remains traceable to the originating evidence records in the full graph, although provenance nodes are omitted here for clarity.

### Short Title

Oolong gut-brain-axis path

### Caption Draft

**Figure C06-2. A gut-brain-axis subgraph linking oolong tea polyphenols to cognition-related outcomes.**  
This story-specific subgraph captures a reported mechanistic route from oolong tea polyphenols to cognition-associated phenotypes. The graph links oolong tea polyphenols to microbiota changes involving Muribaculaceae, Clostridia_UCG-014, and Desulfovibrio, together with downstream signals including LPS, SCFAs, and glutamate. These microbial and metabolite shifts are connected to reduced microglial activation, intestinal barrier protection, and support of synaptic plasticity markers including BDNF, PSD-95, and SYN. The final phenotype node represents cognitive impairment-related outcomes observed in a preclinical circadian-disruption model. This subgraph demonstrates that the same graph framework can represent gut-brain-axis mechanisms, not only metabolic pathways.

## Figure C06-3

### Full Title

Tea polysaccharides form a multi-branch microbiota-mediated mechanism family spanning thermogenesis, bile-acid remodeling, and oxidative-neural protection

### Short Title

Polysaccharide mechanism family

### Caption Draft

**Figure C06-3. Tea polysaccharides form a multi-branch microbiota-mediated mechanism family in the refined C06 graph.**  
Unlike a single linear path, tea polysaccharide records organize into a branched mechanism family centered on microbiota-mediated host regulation. One branch links tea polysaccharides to Bifidobacterium, butyrate, and thermogenic programming involving PGC-1alpha and UCP1, terminating in adipose browning and obesity-related phenotypes. A second branch connects yellow tea polysaccharides to microbiota remodeling, altered conjugated and non-12OH bile acids, and host pathways involving FXR-FGF15-FGFR4-ASBT suppression and alternative bile-acid synthesis, ultimately linking to NAFLD-related phenotypes. A third branch connects tea flower polysaccharides to changes in the Firmicutes/Bacteroidota balance and Lactobacillus, together with NF-kB suppression and antioxidant enzyme restoration, converging on oxidative-damage and microglial-neuroinflammatory phenotypes. Together, these branches suggest that tea polysaccharides represent a mechanism-rich family rather than a single-function component class.

### Result Paragraph Draft

The most graph-native pattern in the current C06 dataset emerged from the tea polysaccharide subgraph. Rather than forming a single linear mechanism, tea polysaccharide records organized into a multi-branch family spanning at least three host programs. The first branch linked Bifidobacterium and butyrate to thermogenic programming and adipose browning. The second linked yellow tea polysaccharides to bile-acid remodeling and NAFLD-related phenotypes through FXR-FGF15-FGFR4-ASBT-related regulation. The third linked tea flower polysaccharides to shifts in the Firmicutes/Bacteroidota balance and Lactobacillus, together with NF-kB suppression and antioxidant restoration, connecting microbiota remodeling to oxidative and neuroinflammatory phenotypes. This structure illustrates a key strength of the graph: it can represent a reusable family of microbiota-mediated mechanisms centered on one tea component class while preserving branch-specific biology.
